that nobody is perfect and that taking responsibility is important. These everyday moments may seem 
small, but they shape a child’s character over time.
Another big part of fatherhood is support. Growing up can be confusing and stressful. Kids deal with 
school pressure, friendships, and sometimes personal struggles. A father who listens without judging 
and gives helpful advice can make a big difference. Even something simple, like asking how their day 
was or helping with homework, shows care. When fathers attend school events, sports games, or 
performances, it makes children feel proud and valued. Support builds confidence and helps kids 
believe they can reach their goals.
Fatherhood also means setting boundaries. While it is important to be kind and loving, it is also 
important to teach discipline. Rules help children understand right from wrong and keep them safe. A 
good father explains why rules matter instead of just giving punishments. This helps children 
understand the reasons behind decisions and learn self-control.
Most importantly, fatherhood is about love. Love can be shown in many ways. Some fathers say “I 
love you” often, while others show love by working hard to provide for their family. Love can be seen 
in small actions like cooking dinner, fixing something that is broken, or giving advice when it is 
needed. When children feel loved, they feel safe and secure. That feeling stays with them as they 
grow up.
In conclusion, fatherhood is more than just a title. It is a daily responsibility that includes teaching, 
guiding, supporting, and loving a child. A father helps shape who a child becomes in the future. Even 
though no father is perfect, the effort and care they give can leave a lasting impact. Fatherhood is 
one of the most important roles in a family and in a child’s life.

CONTEST DETAILS
Each student is asked to write a 3000 character (or less) essay on the theme “What My Father or
an Important Father-Figure Means to Me,” describing their relationship with their father or father-
figure. Students should share their true feelings. Provide examples of joys, frustrations or special
memories they have as well as commenting about what they would most like to see in their
relationship. A father-figure can be a stepdad, grandfather, uncle, coach, pastor/rabbi/shaman, 
God, or an ideal vision of a father. 
CRITERIA FOR ESSAY JUDGING
Submissions will be evaluated according to grade level. Judging criteria will consist of:
1. How the essay describes the relationship.
2. Detail and stories that demonstrate the character of the father/father-figure.
3. Honesty, clarity, simplicity of writing.
4. Overall grade level appropriate writing skill.
JUDGING PROCESS
1. Essays will be divided into the 13 grade levels (K-12).
2. Volunteers will use the above judging criteria, reading every essay. Top essays from
the first round will be sent to a final essay reading session.
3. At the final essay reading, volunteers will then select 10 essays per grade. Up to 130
essay semi-finalists will receive special recognition.
4. The top 13 essay writers will be invited to the IFI Fatherhood Dinner Celebration.
5. All entrants will receive a Certificate of Participation.
